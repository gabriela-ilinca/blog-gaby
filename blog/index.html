The Idolization of Stars

June 17 2018 by Gaby

This blog post shows a few different types of content that’s supported and styled with Bootstrap. Basic typography, images, and code are all supported.

P.T. Barnum starred in a recent musical known as The Greatest Showman, which portrayed him as a crusader for social justice and a cheerleader for outsiders, with its bearded women, tattooed men, little people and conjoined twins.

The problem is, the real-life P.T. Barnum was actually a terrible human being. Beneath his carefully constructed façade, he made his career by exploiting African-Americans and people with disabilities.

For example, he bought a blind, severely paralyzed African-American woman and advertised her as “the Greatest Natural and National Curiosity in the World.” Barnum claimed she was 161 years old (she was half that). He reportedly made up to $1,500 a week displaying her.

She didn’t receive a penny.

When she died, Barnum held a public autopsy and charged spectators to watch.

Barnum’s abuse of animals was second only to his merciless treatment of humans. He chartered a vessel to capture nine elephants from Sri Lanka, including one calf. They were all confined to a cramped compartment on a ship — without any fresh air — for four months.

One died on board.

As if that weren’t despicable enough, he also jammed hot pokers up elephants’ trunks in a control technique called the “burning method.”

At one point, Barnum acquired two beluga whales and imprisoned them in a tank in the basement of his New York museum. Both were dead within two days.

If The Greatest Showman is a cheerleader for outsiders, that can only be good. However, let’s not airbrush history.

To really do Barnum’s story justice, they would be better off making a horror movie.

This Decade's Most Efficient Marketing Scheme

December 23, 2013 by Jacob

In 2005, the manufacturing company set up a promotional stunt at a Vancouver bus stop.

They were so confident about the strength of its bulletproof glass that they put three million dollars inside it.

If anyone can break the case open the money is theirs for the taking.

Passers-by were allowed to do some crazy things to the glass, such as getting a running start to kick the case and even taking a sledgehammer to it.
